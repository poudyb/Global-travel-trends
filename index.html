Global Tourism

This visualization shows trends in worldwide tourism, as measure by inbound arrivals and outbound departures for tourism purposes. Scroll down to learn more about how tourism has changed over the last 25 years.
Our data begins in 1995, when worldwide tourism was centralized in Europe.
Over the past 25 years, we've seen growth in China, Southeast Asia, and Latin America. Pause to watch the bubbles change size on the map, representing these increases in international tourist visitors in each location as time goes by.
Out of the top 25 tourist destinations, there has been a rapid and steady increase, with notable dips in 2001 after the 9/11 attacks and in the 2008 financial crisis. Why has international tourism increased so rapidly?
In South Korea, the government has made a concerted effort to export its culture and attract tourists. Inbound tourism grew 450% from 1995 to 2018. Given its current status as an entertainment export powerhouse, with the global “K-pop” phenomenon driving a >$5 billion Korean music industry today and Korean films raking in awards like the Oscar for Best Picture, Korea also tells a compelling story about the impact government spending and cultural exports can have on a country’s tourism industry. It also provides an informative view on the type of impact epidemics can have on tourism locally, based on its 2015 MERS epidemic. This is a potential hint of what the world may face during the COVID pandemic. Scroll down for more detailed information on Korea's journey.
We can see notable impacts of world events and government initiatives in Korea's inbound tourist arrivals.
